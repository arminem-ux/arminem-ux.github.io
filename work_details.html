About The Company: Engine Yard is a platform as a service (PaaS) provider that automates, configures and deploys applications in the cloud. The platform uses Java, Ruby on Rails, PHP and Node.js for both large-scale and small-scale cloud deployments. Cloud application developers and architects choose Engine Yard for a number of reasons, including self-containment, independent infrastructure and control. Therefore, applications that use Engine Yard's platform are independently contained and do not share infrastructure or virtual machines.

@Margaret Rouse

About The Application: Originally the Health Check application was part of a bigger platform, in the process we separated it to be a stand alone app due to production costs. The main purpose of the application then became to draw the target audience to the main “paid” Saas portal. So basically it became a freebie that on a certain level prompts the customer to register on the main Saas while giving moderate value. The App was designed to give Ruby developers and dev team managers the ability to check their code for multiple vulnerabilities and while providing some low level insights the app prompted them to sign up and get solution options.

About the process: Due to short time frame we combined wireframing and value proposition user interviewing stages…the plus side was that the product was designated for high to mid level ruby programmers and managers and while working in enterprise level factory model company can sometimes become overwhelming in this case it came in handy…we had an unlimited number of potential customers to test and interview with ( people not related to the project). The process was divided into weekly sprints. The first stage of wireframing and data gathering took a week and reviled that 45% of interviewees found the concept useless and even frustrating…10% didn’t see where they can use it…yet the 35% found it a handy low level tool…that was enough for the stakeholders to give the green light. Weekly testing sessions proved to be very productive (while in most cases that can add quite a bit to the budget). See below screens from 4 weeks of iterations and testing. Interviews showed that our customers mainly preferred the dark theme and large icon/button like actionables ( some said that gave them that little game like feeling)

About challenges: This was my first time I faced an ethical dilemma: I had couple of requests to do dark UX (that is make/force the user to click unintentionally) Fortunately, we found a compromise and I added the option to “learn more” on the second level information flow. See the screens below.
